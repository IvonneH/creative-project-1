Family Reunion

Tahoe is the taste of trees towering above me
The smell of napping, sun-cooked needles twice as long as my hand.

My hand hits ice cold water,
As we try to catch shadow-shy fish.
But their tiny bendy bodies slip silently between my fingers.

My fingers grasp the top bunk,
And I pull myself up.
My cousins are asleep,
So I welcome the warming covers
Keeping out cold coming creeping from an open window,
I feel the night’s breath whisper in my ear and glide by my cheek.

My cheek is all red, and my forehead and nose,
From a day spent climbing rocks under a blinding sun,
A happy hatlessness and a shameless shoelessness
Developing sunburns and calluses,
Having fun. I’ve won.
I’m a happy Hilton, perfectly at home here.
I’m a cousin, a niece, a granddaughter, a sister.
Gathered on the gravel beach, I see my cousins. I smile.

My smile sticks like wet sand
For the remainder of the reunion.
Then I realize it’s time to return home.
At a run, I race to my cousin-best-friend.
It could be a year till I see her again.
My feet bounding, my heart pounding
My arms wrap around, we cling
To each other, saying goodbye. I sigh.
A feeling of family is rooted to this spot,
It beats in my heart.
It tickles my face and numbs my hand.
Blowing in the breeze and burning my skin,
It swims past my fingers and into my soul.

Ponderosa

I wrap my arms around yam-colored peanut-brittle bark
Because my brother told me to smell it. It’s flaky and warm.
With a deep breath, I find it’s sweet. like vanilla. or butterscotch.
Pulling away, dismayed, I find shiny, sticky sap on my fingers,
And I see little ants crawling on the orange bark. Egh.
Maybe I shouldn't have touched the tree.
Just smelled it.

My other brother says the needles taste the way they smell.
He knows from experience.
I ponder the Ponderosa.
Wouldn't it be wonderful if the bark tasted the way it smelled?
With melt in your mouth goodness,
It would be as sweet as maple syrup.

Oh, what a tasty treat is this beautiful day!
Mighty pines are scattered about towers of orange rock
Which are bumpy and lopsided.
Like popsicles melting in the summer sun.
I see people far below in the canyon, as little as ants.
The breeze brings me scents of pine sap,
And in the deep blue expanse above me hang wispy tufts of cotton candy.

Rest

Eyes closed. Lying on the soft spring lawn.
In the shade.

My Mountains

I push off the trampoline as hard as I can.
Determined to launch my sister higher than ever before.
We are both grinning,
I am pushed by an unexpectedly strong gust of wind.
I look where it came from, my grand gray mountains.
A wave of clouds splash over those northern mountains,
Preparing to flood the valley in slow motion.
The wind ushers in this storm,
Which will soon fill the valley to it’s brim, and run over.

My mom drives me to school,
And I turn to look out the back window.
There I see my morning mountain,
That launches the sun, but only when I'm at the house.
Maybe it does it for me.
Through the front windshield,
I can barely see a mountain through the haze.
To the right, Timpanogos.
To the left, a shining lake with distant mountains behind.
I’m surrounded.
It makes me feel protected.

I run outside at the word fire.
My mountain on fire.
An ever growing gray cloud gains ground,
And fills the sky.
Airborne firefighters fly by, dumping retardant.
But I see a burst of flame on the smoke’s edge.
Seemingly smaller than a birthday candle.
A pine tree exploding.
Those trees become black as my scuffed dress shoes as their
Needles and bark are shred into an upward surge of smoke.
But my mountain is steady.

Adrift

Rippled sunlight rests on rocks
Beneath the lake’s glassy surface,
On which lies reflected colors fled
From pine trees and stones.
Snow covered mountains and a wide blue sky,
I give it a glance
Before stretching Safari out of its corner
Placing its page
In front of the forest
Displayed on my desktop.
With so much to see,
You Tube, Facebook, Studio C,
I become lost on the lake.
A few clicks bring me Netflix.
I get settled on the bed
And my sister picks the show.
I dive, go below, completely submerged.
I come up for air after three episodes.
But I have homework.
My sister leaves.
Alone on the lake, I sail up to Google docs.
With feet firm on the shore,
I research and write.
It’s midnight.
Good night.
I exit the window,
To see the forest once more,
And log off.
